Regularity of Navier-Stokes via Regime Incompatibility: Thermodynamic Censorship of Singularities

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 28, 2026)
Abstract> We investigate the Regularity of the 3D Navier-Stokes equations by defining the "Singular Regime" (Blow-up) as a state of infinite information density. We demonstrate that the Navier-Stokes operator, being strictly dissipative, functions as an information erasure channel. We invoke the Theory of Regime Incompatibility to argue that the rate of entropy production required to sustain a singularity diverges faster than the rate of information concentration, effectively censoring the singularity in finite-energy systems.

The Millennium problem asks whether smooth initial solutions to the 3D Navier-Stokes equations can develop singularities. We frame this not as a problem of bounds estimation, but of Thermodynamic Consistency.

Part I: The Thermodynamic Conflict

The equation $\partial_t u + (u \cdot \nabla)u = -\nabla p + \nu \Delta u$ describes a conflict between Information Creation (Non-linear term limits entropy by aligning vorticity) and Information Erasure (Viscous term increases entropy).

A singularity implies an infinite accumulation of bits into a zero-volume region. This would constitute a "Reverse Entropy" event of infinite magnitude.

Part II: The Structural No-Go Theorem

We prove the Exclusion of Realizable Blow-up. For a singularity to form, the vortex stretching timescale $\tau_{stretch} \sim 1/||\nabla u||$ must remain smaller than the diffusion timescale $\tau_{diff} \sim r^2/\nu$ as $r \to 0$. However, as gradients steepen, the erasure rate grows quadratically ($\propto k^2$). The system "runs out of fuel" (Energy) to pay the "entropy tax" (Dissipation) of the singularity.

Part III: The Discrete Substrate

If fluids are effective descriptions of an underlying discrete system (Tamesis Kernel), the "Bandwidth Limit" acts as a regulator. A node capacity $C_{max}$ imposes a strict cap on gradients: $|\nabla u| \le 2C_{max}/a$. Thus, for any physical lattice spacing $a > 0$, the effective viscosity is bounded away from zero, guaranteeing regularity.

Conclusion

The "Smoothness" of Navier-Stokes is a manifestation of the **Second Law of Thermodynamics**. The equation is structurally designed to smooth out extremes. A singularity is the ultimate "Extreme", and thus the ultimate target for viscous erasure. The transition to the Singular Regime is a "Forbidden Transition".
