No-Go Theorems for Unified Field Theories: The Information Catastrophe

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 23, 2026)
We present a series of No-Go theorems derived from the Theory of Regime Incompatibility. We prove that any theory attempting to unify a continuous metric field (GR) with a discrete quantum field (QFT) in a single Hilbert space necessarily leads to an "Information Catastrophe": the required information density to describe the interface exceeds the Bekenstein bound. This result suggests that "Quantum Gravity" as generally conceived is not just difficult, but physically impossible.

Historically, physics has progressed by unification (Maxwell, Glashow-Weinberg-Salam). This has created an expectation that gravity must follow suit. However, the conceptual gap between Geometry and Quanta is fundamentally different from previous unifications.

Theorem I: The Metric Uncertainty Limit

Let $g_{\mu\nu}$ be a field possessing simultaneous continuous symmetries (Diff) and quantum uncertainties.

Theorem: A dynamic metric cannot sustain quantum superposition below the Planck scale without violating Causality.

Proof Sketch: A superposition of metrics $|g_1\rangle + |g_2\rangle$ implies a superposition of light cones. Two events $A$ and $B$ can be timelike separated in $g_1$ but spacelike in $g_2$. The causal structure becomes indefinite, making the definition of local observables impossible.

Theorem II: Because Bekenstein

The most potent objection comes from Holography.

The Information Catastrophe Principle: $$ I_{unified} \gg A / 4l_P^2 $$

If we assign a quantum degree of freedom to every point in a continuous spacetime manifold (as required by a naive quantization of GR fields to match standard model precision), the information content in any volume $V$ scales as $V$. However, the Bekenstein bound restricts the physical information to the area $A$.

For any macroscopic volume, $V \gg A$ (in Planck units). Thus, a unified theory describing "bulk" quantum gravity contains vastly more degrees of freedom than can physically exist. This implies that the bulk degrees of freedom are redundant or gauge artifacts, yet QFT treats them as real.

Theorem III: The Discrete-Continuous Gap

QFT relies on a separable Hilbert space. GR relies on a continuous manifold (uncountable). While one can define QFT on a manifold, one cannot generate the manifold from the QFT operators without introducing a "pre-geometry" or breaking the continuum hypothesis.

Conclusion

The dream of "quantizing gravity" is akin to "digitizing water dynamics" at the atomic level: the hydrodynamics (GR) is an emergent property that loses meaning at the scale of the atoms (Quanta). There is no quantum theory of water waves; there is a quantum theory of atoms, from which waves emerge. Similarly, there is no Quantum Gravity; there is a Quantum Theory of Matter, from which Spacetime emerges.
