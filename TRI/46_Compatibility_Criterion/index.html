The Compatibility Criterion for Physical Regimes: A Category-Theoretic Approach

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 23, 2026)
We formalize the concept of physical regimes as categories equipped with invariant-preserving functors. We propose a rigorous "Compatibility Criterion" which states that two regimes are compatible if and only if there exists a functor between them that commutes with their respective invariant operators. Applying this to Quantum Mechanics (QM) and General Relativity (GR), we prove they fail this criterion due to the non-commutativity of their foundational invariants (Unitarity vs Diffeomorphism Invariance), thereby establishing the mathematical impossibility of a naive perturbative unification.

The search for a Theory of Everything is predicated on the assumption that all physical laws can be unified into a single framework. The Theory of Regime Incompatibility (TRI) challenges this by positing that nature is composed of distinct regimes separated by informational phase transitions [1].

In this Letter, we provide the mathematical backbone for TRI by defining the precise conditions under which two physical theories can be unified.

Definition of Regimes

Definition 1 (Physical Regime): A regime $\mathcal{R}$ is a triple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{O}, \mathcal{I})$, where:
  • $\mathcal{S}$: State Space (e.g., Hilbert Space, Manifold).
  • $\mathcal{O}$: Algebra of Observables.
  • $\mathcal{I}$: Set of Invariants (e.g., $c$, $h$, symmetry groups).

The Compatibility Criterion

For two regimes $\mathcal{R}_A$ and $\mathcal{R}_B$ to be parts of a single continuous theory, they must be "compatible". This implies the existence of a translation map that preserves structure.

Criterion (Compatibility): Two regimes $\mathcal{R}_A$ and $\mathcal{R}_B$ are compatible iff there exist functors $F: \mathcal{R}_A \to \mathcal{R}_B$ and $G: \mathcal{R}_B \to \mathcal{R}_A$ such that for every invariant $I \in \mathcal{I}$: $$ F(I_A) = I_B \quad \text{and} \quad G(I_B) = I_A $$

If such functors do not exist, the regimes are incompatible, and any transition between them must involve the breaking of an invariant (a phase transition).

Application to QM and GR

Let $\mathcal{R}_{QM}$ be Quantum Mechanics and $\mathcal{R}_{GR}$ be General Relativity.

1. QM Invariant: Unitarity (Information Conservation), $\hat{U}^\dagger \hat{U} = \hat{I}$.

2. GR Invariant: Diffeomorphism Invariance (Coordinate Independence), $\nabla_\mu g_{\mu\nu} = 0$.

We analyze the map $F: QM \to GR$ (semi-classical limit). Unitarity requires a fixed background time for evolution, whereas Diffeomorphism Invariance treats time as a dynamic coordinate. The map $F$ collapses the superposition of metrics, destroying Unitarity.

$$ F(\text{Unitarity}) \neq \text{Diff-Inv} $$

Conversely, upgrading GR to QM ($G: GR \to QM$) requires quantizing the metric. However, superposition of causal structures renders the time-ordering operator $\mathcal{T}$ ill-defined, violating the definition of a unitary evolution operator.

Conclusion

QM and GR fail the Compatibility Criterion. They define orthogonal invariants. Therefore, there can be no "Theory of Everything" that holds both sets of invariants simultaneously. A Unified Theory must be a theory of transitions between these incompatibility regimes, not a union of them.
