The Computational Architecture of Reality: The Tamesis Kernel and the Emergence of Spacetime

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 28, 2026)
We present a unified model of the universe as a distributed computational system operating on a discrete informational graph, termed the "Tamesis Kernel". We demonstrate that the smooth manifold of General Relativity (spacetime) is not fundamental but an emergent statistical property of the graph in the thermodynamic limit. We redefine Gravity not as a force, but as an Interface Protocol that mediates information exchange between distinct quantum subsystems. This framework resolves the conflict between Locality and Continuity (TRI) and provides a rigorous derivation for the "Realizability Filter" that selects physical laws.

The search for a unified theory has historically been framed as a geometric problem: how to reconcile the curvature of spacetime with the linearity of Hilbert space. We argue this is a category error. Reality is neither purely geometric nor purely algebraic; it is computational.

Part I: The Tamesis Kernel

We postulate that the fundamental substrate of reality is a dynamic graph $G = (V, E)$, where $V$ represents processing units (nodes) and $E$ represents causal relations (edges).

This graph, the Tamesis Kernel, operates according to three primary axioms:

  1. Discreteness: Information is quantized. There is a minimum bit size ($l_p$).
  2. Locality: Nodes only interact with neighbors. $c$ is the max propagation speed.
  3. Finitude: The total information content is bounded by the Bekenstein Limit.

Part II: The Emergence of Spacetime

Spacetime as described by General Relativity is the "Hydrodynamic Limit" of the Kernel. Just as water appears smooth despite being made of discrete molecules, spacetime appears continuous because we observe it at scales $L \gg l_p$.

$$ g_{\mu\nu} \approx \langle \text{Graph Topology} \rangle_{ensemble} $$ The metric tensor is the statistical average of the edge density.

Part III: Gravity as Interface

In this model, Gravity is not a force field. It is the **Cost of Information Transfer**. Mass dilates time (slows down the clock) because high information density ($H$) creates network congestion.

The Equivalence Principle Reinterpreted: Acceleration is indistinguishable from Gravity because both represent a change in the frame of reference relative to the information flow.

Part IV: The Realizability Filter

The laws of physics are not arbitrary. They are the "driver software" allowed to run on the Kernel. Any law that requires exponential computing resources (like solving NP-Hard problems) is blocked by the **Realizability Filter** ($ \Phi_{\mathcal{R}} $).

This explains the "unreasonable effectiveness" of polynomial math in physics: the universe filters out everything else.

Conclusion

The universe is a self-optimizing code. We have moved from a "Block Universe" (Static Geometry) to a "Processing Universe" (Dynamic Computation). This architecture unifies the discrete foundations (TRI) with the emergent complexity of the Standard Model.
