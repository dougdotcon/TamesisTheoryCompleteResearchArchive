The Theory of Structural Solvability: A Meta-Theoretical Classification of Decision Problems

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 28, 2026)
DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18407409
We propose a meta-theoretical framework to classify mathematical conjectures based on the topological rigidity of their solution spaces. We distinguish between Class A (Structurally Rigid), where local invariants enforce global uniqueness, and Class B (Structurally Universal), where the solution space is dominated by asymptotic statistical laws. We identify a fundamental "Density Obstruction" that prevents Class A methods (Geometric Exclusion) from resolving Class B problems. Finally, we demonstrate that the resolution of Class B problems (e.g., P vs NP, Riemann Hypothesis) requires a perspective shift from Internal Deduction to External Thermodynamic Selection.

Mathematical problems are traditionally classified by their logical complexity or computational cost. We propose a third axis: Structural Solvability. This axis measures the efficacy of "Exclusion Arguments"—proofs that proceed by ruling out all alternative configurations until only one remains.

Part I: The Taxonomy of Difficulty

We define two distinct classes of mathematical objects based on their response to local deformation.

1. Class A (Structurally Rigid)

A problem belongs to Class A if its solution space $\Omega$ admits a set of discrete invariants that collapse $\Omega$ to a finite set. The mechanism is Geometric Rigidity.

Example: The Poincaré Conjecture. Ricci Flow smooths out irregularities, forcing the manifold into a standard sphere. The "solution" is uniquely determined by the topology.

2. Class B (Structurally Universal)

A problem belongs to Class B if $\Omega$ remains infinite-dimensional and exhibits Universality. Generic configurations converge to a statistical limit distribution rather than a unique structure.

Example: The Riemann Hypothesis. The critical line is an attractor of a chaotic operator class, but individual zeros are statistically distributed (GUE), not geometrically fixed.

Part II: The Structural Barrier

We conjecture that methods successful in Class A (e.g., surgery, contraction mapping) face a Structural Mismatch when applied to Class B.

The Density Obstruction Conjecture:
In a Class B space, the set of configuration "defects" is statistically dense. For any local invariant $I$, the distribution of $I$ converges to a universal limit law.

Consequence: One cannot use $I$ to distinguish "Structure" from "Randomness" unless the deviation is macroscopic. This generalizes the "Natural Proofs" barrier (Razborov & Rudich) to structural topology. If you simplify the Riemann Operator to be rigid (Class A), it ceases to be chaotic (Class B).

Part III: Thermodynamic Selection

If geometry cannot "close" Class B problems, are they undecidable? We argue they are logically undecidable via internal geometry but Realizability Constrained.

The Principle of Structural Selection

For Class B problems, the "solution" is not a unique geometric object, but a Stable Statistical Phase chosen by thermodynamic constraints.

Solving a Class B problem implies identifying the Lyapunov Function (Entropy/Cost) that the system minimizes. For the Riemann Hypothesis, the Maximum Entropy Principle selects the GUE distribution (Critical Line) as the only stable configuration.

Conclusion

The "drought" in solving Millennium Problems is a category error. We are applying Class A tools (Topology, Algebra) to Class B objects (Turbulence, Complexity, Chaos). The Theory of Structural Solvability creates the map to identify the correct methodological approach: Geometry for Rigid systems, Thermodynamics for Universal systems.
