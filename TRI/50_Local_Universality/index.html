Local Universality: Constants as Regime Invariants

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 23, 2026)
We propose a reinterpretation of the fundamental constants of nature ($c, G, \hbar$) not as global properties of the universe, but as local invariants characterizing specific physical regimes. We demonstrate that universality is a local phenomenon: laws appear universal only because terrestrial experiments are confined to a single, stable regime. This "Local Universality" hypothesis resolves the fine-tuning problem by demoting constants to environmental parameters, subject to evolution across regime transitions.

Are the laws of physics the same everywhere? The standard answer assumes Global Universality. However, the Theory of Regime Incompatibility suggests that laws are tied to the structural regime of the substrate.

The Contextuality of Constants

Consider the speed of sound $v_s$ in a fluid. It is constant and isotropic within the fluid, acting as a "local light speed". However, it loses meaning if the fluid boils into gas. We argue $c$ is analogous.

$$ c_{vac} = \text{Invariant of } \mathcal{R}_{EM} $$

If the electromagnetic vacuum were to undergo a phase transition (e.g., vacuum decay), the value of $c$ would change or become undefined. Thus, $c$ is a Local Invariant of our current vacuum phase.

The constants are "flags" indicating which regime is active. Where $\hbar \neq 0$, we are in QM. Where $1/c \neq 0$, we are in GR. The limit $c \to \infty$ is not just a mathematical approximation; it describes a transition to a Non-Relativistic regime where causality is instantaneous.

Implications for Fine Tuning

The Fine-Tuning Problem asks why constants have the specific values they do. Under Local Universality, this is a selection bias. We occupy a specific regime ($\mathcal{R}_{Anthropic}$) defined by this set of invariants. Other regimes with different constants likely exist (e.g., pre-inflationary vacuum), but they are causally disconnected or structurally incompatible with biological information processing.

Conclusion

Universality is local. Physics is an archipelago of laws, not a single continent. Our task is to map the shoreline of our island.
