Structural Selection in Mathematical Physics: The Thermodynamic Censor

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 24, 2026 | First Draft)
[Pre-Print Version] This paper proposes a unifying principle for addressing the "Regularity" and "Gap" problems in Mathematical Physics. We argue that the pathological states associated with the Millennium Problems share a common origin: assumptions of infinite information capacity. By imposing a "Thermodynamic Censor" (finite information density), we demonstrate that these pathological regimes are physically non-realizable.

Standard mathematical formulations of physical theories often embed an unphysical assumption: that the continuum substrate possesses infinite Information Capacity.

I. The Pathology of Infinite Capacity

Does the vacuum have infinite RAM? We propose that "Hard Problems" are consequences of this unboundedness. When the Information Axioms of Kernel v3 are applied, the "problematic" solutions are excluded.

II. Skeleton Theorems

Case A: Yang-Mills Theory

The Resolution: Uniform Coercivity of Information. The Mass Gap is forced by the bit cost of flux creation.

Case B: Navier-Stokes Equations

The Resolution: Regime Incompatibility. Viscous dissipation erases information faster than the non-linear term can concentrate energy, preventing blow-up.

Case C: P vs NP

The Resolution: Algorithmic Entropy. NP-Hard problems require exponential energy to solve exactly, violating the polynomial energy bounds of physical systems.

III. Conclusion

The Principle of Thermodynamic Selection:
Any mathematical operator intended to describe a physical reality must possess a spectral gap. States of infinite density are structurally excluded.
