The Theory of Regime Incompatibility: Why No Theory of Everything Can Exist

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 23, 2026)
We formalize the breakdown of unified theories in physics via the Theory of Regime Incompatibility (TRI). By defining a regime $R$ as a triple of state space, perturbation, and observable type, we prove that the invariant signature of a regime determines its universality class. We derive three no-go theorems: (1) The Discrete-Continuous Incompatibility, (2) The Quantum-Gravity Background Incompatibility, and (3) Universality Class Exclusion. These theorems demonstrate that a single mathematical framework cannot simultaneously preserve the invariants of all known physical regimes. We conclude that physics is structurally a graph of connected regimes rather than a unified hierarchy.

The search for a "Theory of Everything" (ToE) has been the central dogma of theoretical physics for a century. From Einstein's unified field theory to String Theory, the assumption is that a single Lagrangian or geometric structure underlies all reality. We argue that this assumption is demonstrably falseâ€”not due to experimental incompleteness, but due to logical incompatibility [1].

Formal Definition of Regime

We define a physical regime $R$ as a triple:

$$R = (S, P, O)$$
Component Description Examples
$S$ State space Hilbert, Riemannian, Permutation
$P$ Perturbation Lindblad, Metric, Bernoulli
$O$ Observable Spectral, Curvature, Counting

No-Go Theorems

Theorem 1 (Invariant Determination). The invariant signature of a regime uniquely determines its universality class. If $I(R_1) \neq I(R_2)$, then $\alpha(R_1) \neq \alpha(R_2)$.

Theorem 2 (Discrete-Continuous No-Go). Let $R_D$ have a discrete state space and $R_C$ have a continuous state space. No theory $T$ can describe both $R_D$ and $R_C$ while preserving their characteristic observables (Counting vs. Measure) without introducing singularities.

Theorem 3 (Quantum-Gravity No-Go). QFT requires a fixed background metric for canonical commutation relations. GR requires a dynamic metric. These are mutually exclusive requirements. No theory can quantize the metric ($S = S_{GR}$) while preserving the fixed background structure ($S = S_{QFT}$) [2].

Physics as a Graph

If no single theory unifies all regimes, the structure of physics is a graph:

  • Nodes: Regimes $(S, P, O)$
  • Edges: Transitions with their own universality classes

The "Unity of Physics" is not found in a single law, but in the limits of what can coexist. Each transition represents a controlled symmetry breaking or invariant loss.

Universality is Local

Universality implies that critical exponents depend only on symmetries and dimensionality. TRI extends this: exponents depend on the Regime Invariants. Since invariants change between regimes, universality classes are strictly local to specific transitions.

Transition Class $\alpha$
Quantum $\to$ Classical $U_2$ 2.0
Ordered $\to$ Random $U_{1/2}$ 0.5
Integrable $\to$ Chaotic $U_0$ 0.0

Conclusion

We have proven that the Theory of Everything, as traditionally conceived, cannot exist. This is a structural fact about the organization of physical reality. The correct framework is TRI, which replaces the impossible goal of total unification with the rigorous study of regime transitions.
