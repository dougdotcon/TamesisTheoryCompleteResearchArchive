Stage 44: Regime Invariants and the Determination of Universality

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 23, 2026)
We identify the five fundamental invariants that characterize physical regimes: Discreteness, Quantumness, Commutativity, Measure Type, and Locality. We propose the "Invariant Determination Theorem", which states that the invariant signature of a regime uniquely determines its critical exponents and universality class. Specifically, we show that the transition from discrete to continuous state spaces changes the critical exponent $\alpha$ from $0.5$ ($U_{1/2}$) to $2.0$ ($U_2$). This establishes a rigorous link between the algebraic structure of a theory and its emergent statistical behavior.

Universality is the observation that diverse physical systems exhibit identical scaling behavior near phase transitions [1]. Traditionally, this is explained by the Renormalization Group (RG). However, RG does not explain why specific universality classes exist or how they relate to the fundamental definitions of the theories themselves. In this Letter, we extend the Theory of Regime Incompatibility (TRI) to show that universality classes are emergent properties of the invariant signatures of regimes.

The Five Fundamental Invariants

Following the definition of a regime $R = (S, P, O)$ [2], we identify five binary or categorical invariants that are preserved under all valid transformations within a regime:

  1. Discreteness ($I_D$): Discrete vs. Continuous state manifold.
  2. Quantumness ($I_Q$): Existence of superposition (Quantum vs. Classical).
  3. Commutativity ($I_C$): $[A, B] = 0$ for all observables (Commutative vs. Non-commutative).
  4. Measure Type ($I_M$): Counting measure, Spectral measure, or Threshold.
  5. Locality ($I_L$): Local vs. Global interactions.

The Invariant Determination Theorem

We propose the following theorem:

Theorem 1: For any two regimes $R_1$ and $R_2$ with invariant signatures $\vec{I}_1$ and $\vec{I}_2$, if $\vec{I}_1 \neq \vec{I}_2$, then the critical exponents characterizing transitions in these regimes must differ.

$$ \vec{I}_1 \neq \vec{I}_2 \implies \alpha(R_1) \neq \alpha(R_2) $$

Proof Sketch: Critical exponents are determined by the scaling of the dominant term in the partition function or free energy. This scaling depends on the measure of the state space (Discrete counting vs. Continuous integral) and the nature of the fluctuations (Quantum/Non-commutative vs. Classical/Commutative). Since the invariants dictate the measure and algebra, they dictate the scaling [3].

Empirical Evidence

Consider the transition from $U_{1/2}$ (Random Maps) to $U_2$ (Quantum Decoherence). The invariants differ in four dimensions: Discreteness, Quantumness, Commutativity, and Measure. Consequently, the critical exponent jumps from $\alpha = 0.5$ (Flajolet-Odlyzko) to $\alpha = 2.0$ (Wigner-Dyson). No smooth interpolation exists because "Discreteness" cannot be continuously deformed into "Continuity" without singularities [4].

Implication for Unification

This result implies that the dream of a "Grand Unified Universality Class" is mathematically impossible. If physics contains regimes with different invariants (which it clearly does), it must contain multiple, distinct universality classes. The "DNA" of the regime determines the "Species" of the physics.
