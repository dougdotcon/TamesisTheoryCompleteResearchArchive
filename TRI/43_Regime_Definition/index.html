Stage 43: Formal Definition of Regime

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 23, 2026)
We formalize the concept of a "Physical Regime" as a mathematical triple $R = (S, P, O)$, where $S$ denotes the state space type, $P$ the perturbation structure, and $O$ the observable algebra. This definition allows us to catalog known physical theories into distinct universality classes. We demonstrate that most unification attempts fail because they attempt to merge regimes with incompatible invariants (e.g., discrete vs. continuous state spaces). This formalism lays the groundwork for the Theory of Regime Incompatibility (TRI), proving that no single invariant structure can encompass all observed phenomena.

The term "regime" is ubiquitous in physics, often used informally to delimit the domain of validity of a theory. We differentiate between the "Quantum Regime" and the "Classical Regime", or the "Relativistic Regime" and the "Newtonian Regime" [1]. However, a rigorous mathematical definition of a regime has been lacking. Without such a definition, the question of whether two regimes can be unified remains ill-posed.

In this Letter, we propose a formal definition of a regime that captures its topological and algebraic essence. We argue that the structural invariants of a regime determine its universality class, and that incompatibilities between these invariants are the root cause of the failures of Grand Unified Theories [2, 3].

The Definition

We define a regime $R$ as a triple:

$$R = (S, P, O)$$

where:

1. $S$ (State Space): The mathematical space of allowed states (e.g., Hilbert Space $\mathcal{H}$, Riemannian Manifold $\mathcal{M}$, or Discrete Permutation Set $\Sigma_n$).

2. $P$ (Perturbation Type): The structure of allowed dynamics or errors (e.g., Lindblad operators, Metric perturbations $\delta g_{\mu\nu}$, or Bernoulli bit-flips).

3. $O$ (Observable Type): The algebra of measurable quantities (e.g., Spectral projectors, Local curvature scalars, or Counting measures).

Regime Invariants

Each regime $R$ possesses a set of structural invariants $I(R)$ that do not change under transformations within the regime. Key invariants include:

- Discreteness: Is $S$ continuous or discrete?

- Commutativity: Is the algebra of $O$ commutative?

- Locality: Are perturbations in $P$ local?

Two regimes $R_1$ and $R_2$ are defined to be compatible if and only if they share the same invariants. If $I(R_1) \neq I(R_2)$, no bijective map exists between them that preserves structure, implying that any unification theory must break at least one invariant [4].

Compatibility Analysis

Applying this criterion to the major problems of physics:

1. QFT vs. GR: Incompatible. QFT assumes a fixed background (invariant of $S_{QFT}$), while GR makes the background dynamical (invariant of $S_{GR}$). Furthermore, $O_{QFT}$ is non-commutative while $O_{GR}$ (classically) is commutative.

2. Quantum vs. Classical: Incompatible. The loss of superposition (linearity of $S$) marks a fundamental break in invariants [5].

Conclusion

The $(S, P, O)$ signature determines the universality class of a physical theory. The fact that we observe multiple distinct signatures in nature implies that physics is not a single unified structure, but a graph of regimes. Unification attempts that ignore these invariant incompatibilities are structurally doomed.
