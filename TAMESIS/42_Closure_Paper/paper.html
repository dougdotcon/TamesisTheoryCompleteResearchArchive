An Atlas of Critical Exponents

Tamesis Research Program

Abstract

We present a systematic classification of universality classes in regime transitions, demonstrating that critical exponents encode microscopic mechanisms, not system identities. Through computational exploration (Stages 34-41), we discovered a new universality class U1/2 for discrete-to-random transitions, characterized by the scaling $\phi(c) = (1 + c)^{-1/2}$. We construct a complete atlas of five mechanism-driven universality classes with a decision tree for classification. Crucially, we document extensive negative results that delimit the boundaries of each class. The central insight is epistemological: universality is not global but conditional, emerging only when state space type, perturbation type, and observable align.

1. Introduction

The concept of universality—that different physical systems can share the same critical exponents near phase transitions—is one of the most powerful ideas in statistical mechanics. The renormalization group explains why universality occurs, but leaves open a structural question:

What determines which universality class a system belongs to?

The standard answer focuses on symmetry and dimensionality. We propose a complementary perspective: universality classes are mechanism-driven. The critical exponent $\alpha$ encodes not merely what system is being studied, but how it transitions between regimes.

1.1 The New Claim

Traditional: "System X belongs to class Y."
This work: "System X belongs to class Y because mechanism M dominates."

This is not merely a linguistic shift. It implies that the same system can belong to different universality classes depending on which observable is measured and how perturbations are applied.

2. The U1/2 Discovery

2.1 Setting

Consider a family of maps $f_\epsilon: [n] \to [n]$ interpolating between:

  • Permutation ($\epsilon = 1$): Each element maps to a unique destination
  • Random map ($\epsilon = 0$): Each element maps to a uniform random destination

The interpolation uses $\epsilon = 1 - c/n$, where $c$ is the perturbation parameter.

2.2 Main Result

Theorem (U1/2 Universality)

For the family of perturbed permutations with $\epsilon = 1 - c/n$, the expected fraction of elements in cycles satisfies:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\text{elements in cycles}}{n}\right] = (1 + c)^{-1/2}$$

The exponent $\gamma = 1/2$ is universal, independent of:

  • System size $n$ (after thermodynamic limit)
  • Specific construction of the interpolation
  • Distribution of perturbations (Bernoulli, Poisson, block)

4. Classification Decision Tree

Definition (Mechanism Alignment)

A transition belongs to universality class $U_\alpha$ when three conditions align:

  1. State space type: discrete vs. continuous
  2. Perturbation type: Bernoulli, Lindblad, threshold, Poisson
  3. Observable type: counting-based, purity, spanning, survival

5. Negative Results (Boundaries)

Equally important are the systems where U1/2 does NOT appear:

System Fitted $\alpha$ Why Different
Quantum decoherence 2.0 Continuous Hilbert space, Lindblad
KAM chaos ≈0 Threshold behavior, not gradual
Markov chains 0.047 Different counting (determinism, not cycles)
Neural networks poor fit Continuous weights, optimization dynamics
Hash tables 0.01 Collision counting, not cycle counting
Genetic algorithms 1.56 Exponential survival, not cycle fraction

These negative results delimit the boundaries of U1/2 and confirm that the exponent is mechanism-specific, not system-specific.

6. The Epistemological Shift

Universality is not global; it is conditional.

The traditional view treats universality as a property of systems. Our atlas shows that universality is better understood as a property of mechanisms. The decision tree is not merely a classification tool—it is a map of mechanisms analogous to:

  • RG flow diagrams (but for mechanism types, not couplings)
  • Phase diagrams (but axes are mechanism properties, not thermodynamic variables)

7. What This Work Does NOT Claim

For scientific integrity, we explicitly state what is not claimed:

  • This is not a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis
  • This is not a Theory of Everything
  • U1/2 is not universal for all transitions
  • The atlas is not exhaustive (other classes may exist)

The contribution is precise: a new universality class, an organizational principle, and extensive boundary delimitation through negative results.

8. Conclusion

We have demonstrated that critical exponents in regime transitions encode microscopic mechanisms, not merely system identities. The discovery of U1/2 for discrete-to-random transitions, combined with systematic negative results, establishes a new perspective on universality:

Central Thesis

Universality classes are mechanism-driven. The exponent $\alpha$ emerges from the alignment of state space type, perturbation type, and observable type. When the mechanism changes, the universality class changes—even if the system appears similar.

This principle, now demonstrated across multiple regimes, represents a limited, true, and closeable scientific contribution.
