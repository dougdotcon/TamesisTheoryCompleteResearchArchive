1. The Problem

For $M = SL(2,\mathbb{Z}) \backslash \mathbb{H}$ (modular surface with 1 cusp):

  • Discrete spectrum: Maass cusp forms with $\lambda_n = 1/4 + r_n^2$
  • Continuous spectrum: Eisenstein series $E(z, s)$ for $s \in 1/2 + i\mathbb{R}$

Question: Where does the $T \log T$ term in spectral counting come from?

2. The Scattering Matrix

For $SL(2,\mathbb{Z}) \backslash \mathbb{H}$, the scattering matrix is explicit:

$$\phi(s) = \sqrt{\pi} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(s - \tfrac{1}{2}) \cdot \zeta(2s - 1)}{\Gamma(s) \cdot \zeta(2s)}$$

This encodes how waves scatter off the cusp at infinity.

3. The Scattering Phase

Definition. The scattering phase shift is:

$$\Theta(T) = \frac{1}{\pi} \arg \phi\left(\frac{1}{2} + iT\right)$$

Asymptotic (via Stirling):

$$\Theta(T) = \frac{T}{2\pi} \log\frac{T}{2\pi} - \frac{T}{2\pi} + O(\log T)$$

This is exactly the Riemann-von Mangoldt form!

4. The Target Lemma

Lemma (Cusp Contribution):

For $M = SL(2,\mathbb{Z}) \backslash \mathbb{H}$:

$$N_{\text{total}}(T) = N_{\text{discrete}}(T) + \Theta(T)$$

where:

  • $N_{\text{discrete}}(T) \sim \frac{T^2}{12}$ (pure polynomial - Weyl law)
  • $\Theta(T) \sim \frac{T}{2\pi} \log T$ (logarithmic - cusp contribution)

Corollary: The $T \log T$ term is NOT from the discrete spectrum. It comes entirely from the continuous spectrum (cusp scattering).

6. Mathematical Content

What is known (literature):

  • Discrete spectrum satisfies Weyl law: $N_{\text{Maass}}(T) \sim T^2/12$ (proven)
  • Continuous spectrum contribution equals $\Theta(T)$ (Muller's theory)
  • Stirling gives $\Theta(T) \sim (T/2\pi) \log T$ (standard)

What is being done here:

  • Explicit numerical computation of $\Theta(T)$
  • Isolation of the cusp contribution
  • Verification that log term = scattering phase

7. Why This Matters

The Key Insight:

The $T \log T$ counting in Riemann zeros cannot come from discrete spectrum on a compact space.

It must come from something like "cusp scattering" - information from non-compactness.

This is why the RH operator (if it exists) must live on a non-compact or adelic space.
