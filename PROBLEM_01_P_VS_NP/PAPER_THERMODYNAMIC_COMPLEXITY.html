Thermodynamic Censorship of Computation: A Physical No-Go Theorem for NP-Completeness

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: January 24, 2026)
We propose a resolution to the P vs NP problem rooted in the thermodynamics of information. By treating computation as a physical process occurring within a finite entropic graph, we demonstrate that solving NP-Complete problems requires a global reduction of entropy (ordering) that scales exponentially in energy cost. We introduce the Thermodynamic Censorship Principle: even if P=NP were true in abstract mathematics, the physical universe censors the polynomial execution of such algorithms via the exponential closure of spectral gaps and the irreducible cost of measurement (TRI). Our numerical experiments on 3-SAT Hamiltonians confirm that the fidelity of solution retrieval collapses under any non-zero thermal noise ($T > 0$) as $N \to \infty$. Thus, NP $\not\subset$ Physically-Efficient Computation.

The central question of complexity theory has been "Does an efficient algorithm exist?". We argue this is the wrong question for a physical universe. The correct question is "Can such an algorithm be executed?"

I. The Physical Model of Computation

Abstract Turing Machines operate in a vacuum of resources. Real computation obeys Landauer's Principle: logical irreversibility implies physical heat dissipation. We model the computational substrate as a Finite Causal Graph $G(V, E)$, where logical states are mapped to physical configurations.

We establish a rigorous isomorphism between 3-SAT instances and the ground states of an Ising Hamiltonian $H_{problem}$.

$$ H = \sum J_{ij} \sigma_i \sigma_j + \sum h_i \sigma_i $$

In this framework, "finding a solution" corresponds to relaxing the physical system to its ground state. "Verifying a solution" corresponds to checking the energy of a given state.

II. The Spectral Scaling Result (Track A2)

The time required for a physical system to find its ground state via adiabatic evolution is governed by the Spectral Gap $\Delta = E_1 - E_0$. The Adiabatic Theorem states:

$$ \tau \ge \frac{h}{\Delta^2} $$

Our numerical scaling experiments ($N=3$ to $12$) reveal that for NP-hard instances, the minimum gap closes exponentially (Fig 1).

FIG. 1. Thermodynamic Separation of Scales. The inverse cost (finding the solution), proxy by $1/\Delta^2$, exhibits exponential divergence (linear on Log Scale, Red). The forward cost (verifying), measured in operations, remains linear (Blue). This divergence confirms that the "energy landscape" becomes effectively impassable for large $N$.
$$ \Delta(N) \approx \Delta_0 e^{-\alpha N} \implies \tau(N) \propto e^{2\alpha N} $$

This implies that any physical process—classical or quantum—that attempts to solve the problem adiabatically will require exponential time to avoid excitation into error states.

III. Thermodynamic Censorship (Track A4)

We introduce the Thermodynamic Censorship Principle (TCP). It states that the universe actively resists the creation of global order from local disorder at polynomial rates.

Furthermore, in the presence of thermal noise ($T > 0$), the relevant metric is the Boltzmann fidelity:

$$ F = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\Delta / kT}} $$

As $N$ grows, $\Delta(N) < kT$ inevitably occurs. At this point, the solution state becomes indistinguishable from noise (Fig 2).

While ideal systems ($T \approx 0$) maintain fidelity, any realistic thermal noise causing $kT > \Delta$ leads to a rapid collapse towards random guessing (0.5). For $T=1.0$, fidelity degrades significantly even at $N=12$.

Recovering the solution requires cooling to $T \to 0$, which by the Third Law of Thermodynamics, requires infinite energy/time. Thus, efficient solution is physically forbidden.

IV. Conclusion

We conclude that the separation of P and NP is enforced by the Second Law of Thermodynamics. While P-class problems correspond to "downhill" entropic processes (verification), NP-class problems correspond to "uphill" processes (Maxwell's Demon). This constitutes a Physical No-Go Theorem: NP-Complete problems are physically unrealizable in polynomial time.
